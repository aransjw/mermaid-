Heart Adaptations

Due to Oceanin’s presence and the need to adapt to deep-sea living, the heart differs from a typical human heart by forming a crystalline layer on its surface. This layer results from Oceanin reacting with seawater minerals and is referred to as the “Cardial Crystal Layer.”

Characteristics

  • Thickness: approximately 2–4 mm, covering the entire surface of the heart.
  • Tissue Morphology: Resembles a mixture of pearl and cartilage, transparent and glossy.
  • Properties: Slightly elastic but with extremely high compressive resistance.

Further Info: Crystallization Process

  1. Synthesis: Active Oceanin is synthesized in the bloodstream.
  2. Receptor Binding: Heart epicardial cells express a specialized receptor (OCPR) that recognizes and adsorbs the Oceanin complex, initiating deposition on the surface.
  3. Enzymatic Activation: The Oceanin complex activates an enzyme called Cardio-Crystalase, which refolds the protein chain, serving as a crystallization nucleation template. Minerals from seawater then deposit in an orderly fashion, creating an organic–inorganic composite crystal.

Specific Functions & Rationale

  • High-Pressure Resistance: Prevents myocardial deformation under deep-sea pressures.
  • Electrical Signal Regulation: Enhances synchronization of cardiac electrical potentials, preventing arrhythmias.
  • Bioluminescence Response: Refracts faint deep-sea light to stimulate special photosensitive proteins, regulating heart rate.
  • Immune Barrier: Adsorbs antimicrobial peptides, shielding the heart from deep-sea pathogens.
